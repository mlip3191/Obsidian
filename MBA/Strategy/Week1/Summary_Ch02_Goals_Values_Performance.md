Chapter 2: Goals, Values, and Performance --- Contemporary Strategy
Analysis (Grant, 11th Ed.)

Overview

Chapter 2 addresses a foundational question in strategic management:
what is the firm trying to achieve, and how do we measure success? Grant
begins with the observation that business enterprise is about more than
profit --- entrepreneurs are driven by creativity, autonomy, and impact.
Yet for analytical clarity and competitive survival, long-run profit
maximization serves as the most tractable primary objective.

The chapter examines the tension between shareholder value maximization
and stakeholder value maximization, provides a rigorous toolkit for
measuring and analyzing firm performance, and bridges analytical
precision with broader questions of corporate social responsibility.

Key Concepts and Frameworks

Value Creation

Defined as total customer value minus real production costs ---
encompassing consumer surplus (customers receive more than they pay) and
producer surplus (input owners earn more than minimum). This exceeds
accounting profit. Google\'s free services (Search, Gmail, YouTube) are
cited as an example of enormous value creation with modest revenue
capture.

Shareholder vs. Stakeholder Value

Two governance philosophies are contrasted:

-   Shareholder capitalism: firms should maximize profit for owners,
    relying on competitive markets to align private and social
    interests.

-   Stakeholder theory: firms should serve all constituent groups ---
    employees, customers, suppliers, communities, and shareholders.

Grant acknowledges the appeal of stakeholder theory but notes practical
difficulties (measurement problems, governance challenges). He concludes
that in competitive markets, shareholder and stakeholder interests
largely converge through \"enlightened shareholder value.\"

Profit Measurement

-   Gross profit: revenue minus cost of goods sold

-   Operating profit: gross profit minus operating expenses

-   Net profit: after all deductions including taxes and interest

-   Economic profit (EVA): profit after deducting the cost of capital
    --- the true measure of value creation. Formula: Operating Profit −
    (WACC × Capital Employed)

Discounted Cash Flow (DCF) and Enterprise Value

Firm value equals the present value of future free cash flows,
discounted at the weighted average cost of capital (WACC). This connects
strategy to finance: competitive advantage translates into enterprise
value only when it generates future cash flows exceeding the cost of
capital.

Real Options

Strategic investments create option value --- the right (but not
obligation) to pursue future opportunities. This justifies investing in
R&D, market entry, or platform capabilities even when NPV is initially
negative, because embedded options have significant value.

Carroll\'s Pyramid of Corporate Social Responsibility

Firms must balance profit maximization with legal, ethical, and
philanthropic responsibilities. Empirical evidence suggests that firms
with strong environmental and social performance tend to achieve
superior long-run financial returns.

Main Sections

Strategy as a Quest for Value

Organizational purpose is articulated through mission statements
(Twitter, Nike, Lego). Beneath the diversity of purposes lies a common
denominator: creating value through production (transforming
less-valuable inputs into more-valuable outputs) or commerce
(repositioning goods in space or time).

Shareholder vs. Stakeholder Value

Grant reviews the historical debate. After the 2008--2009 financial
crisis, the reputation of pure shareholder maximization declined. In
2019, 181 leading US CEOs signed the Business Roundtable statement
endorsing stakeholder capitalism. Grant argues that in competitive
markets, the two objectives tend to converge --- companies that treat
stakeholders well tend to outperform over the long run.

Profit, Cash Flow, and Enterprise Value

A detailed treatment of profit measurement, profitability ratios (ROS,
ROE, ROA), and DCF links strategic decisions to firm value. The Diageo
EVA case demonstrates how adopting economic profit analysis transformed
capital allocation decisions.

Beyond Profit --- CSR

Firms must balance profit maximization with their legal, ethical, and
philanthropic responsibilities. Empirical evidence supports the \"doing
well by doing good\" hypothesis: strong ESG performance and superior
long-run financial returns tend to go together.

Beyond Profit --- Real Options

Strategic investments create embedded options that static DCF analysis
misses. Platform investments, R&D programs, and market entry positions
all create option value that justifies investment even when initial NPV
appears negative.

Real-World Examples

-   Diageo (EVA Case): Adopting economic profit analysis revealed that
    prestige aged spirits (Talisker, Dom Perignon) were not creating
    true economic value. Advertising shifted to Smirnoff and Gordon\'s
    Gin. EVA became the primary incentive metric for 1,400 managers, and
    loss-making divisions (Pillsbury, Burger King) were divested.

-   Google/Alphabet: Creates enormous consumer surplus through free
    services far exceeding revenues --- illustrating the gap between
    value creation and profit capture.

-   Henry Ford, Steve Jobs, James Dyson, Mark Zuckerberg: Examples of
    entrepreneurs driven by purpose and creativity beyond profit.

-   Cadbury: Despite exemplary stakeholder relations, weak shareholder
    returns made it vulnerable to acquisition by Kraft Foods ---
    illustrating the survival constraint of profitability.

-   Apple, Amazon, Alibaba, JPMorgan, Roche, Walmart, Toyota: Compared
    on multiple profitability metrics (Table 2.1), showing how
    performance rankings shift depending on the measure used.

-   Business Roundtable (2019): 181 CEOs\' stakeholder commitment
    statement --- a landmark moment in the shareholder vs. stakeholder
    debate.

Critical Takeaways

-   Value creation (consumer surplus + producer surplus) is larger than
    accounting profit and is the ultimate measure of a firm\'s economic
    contribution.

-   Long-run profit maximization is the most tractable strategic
    objective; shareholder and stakeholder interests converge under
    competition.

-   Economic profit (EVA) is a more accurate performance measure than
    accounting profit --- many \"profitable\" firms actually destroy
    value once the cost of capital is accounted for.

-   Enterprise value = present value of future free cash flows; strategy
    creates enterprise value only when cash flows exceed the cost of
    capital.

-   Real options extend the value of strategic investments beyond static
    NPV --- platforms, R&D, and market entry positions create option
    value that DCF alone misses.

-   CSR and financial performance are more complementary than
    conflicting; strong social and environmental practices tend to
    deliver superior long-run returns.

-   Different profitability metrics tell different stories; performance
    analysis requires triangulating across multiple measures.
