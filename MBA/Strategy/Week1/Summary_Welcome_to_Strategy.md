Welcome to Strategic Management! --- Mukund Chari (Introductory Course
Deck)

Overview

This slide deck by Mukund Chari serves as the introductory session for a
Strategic Management course. It orients students to what strategy is and
is not, how strategic decisions are characterized, and the analytical
frameworks and tools the course will employ. The deck combines
conceptual definitions with practical frameworks and establishes the
pedagogical philosophy for the course.

Apophatic Strategy --- What Strategy Is NOT

The deck opens with an \"apophatic\" definition --- defining strategy by
what it is not. Strategy is not:

-   Goal setting or rolling budgets

-   \"Important\" decisions in isolation

-   Inspirational slogans or bromides to work harder

-   Other gifts of current corporate-speak and pop culture

The Steve Jobs example reinforces this. Apple\'s success did not come
from relentlessly focusing on performance metrics, motivating high
performance through incentive systems tied to strategic success factors,
wasting time on distinctions among \"missions,\" \"visions,\" and
\"strategies,\" or using acquisitions to hit strategic growth goals.
Growth was the outcome of successful product development and
accompanying business strategies.

Definition of Strategy

Strategy is defined as the theory (i.e., integrated pattern of
decisions) a firm maintains as to how it captures value, through:

-   Resource allocation priorities

-   Actions in the marketplace

Good strategy therefore involves: understanding an ill-structured
challenge; identifying a non-obvious choice set; analyzing unclear
criteria; and focusing resources and coherent policies to work together
to overcome obstacles.

What Makes a Decision Strategic?

Drawing on Leiblein, Reuer, and Zenger (2018), a decision is strategic
when it involves high interdependence across three dimensions:

-   Inter-decisional interdependence: current decisions affect future
    decisions --- choices are linked across time

-   Inter-temporal interdependence: today\'s choices constrain
    tomorrow\'s options --- the sequencing of decisions matters

-   Inter-actor interdependence: decisions affect and are affected by
    the actions of competitors, partners, and other players

Strategic decisions sit at the intersection of all three types of
interdependence --- they are not just \"important\" decisions, but
decisions with high complexity across these three dimensions.

Business vs. Corporate Strategy

Using Siemens as an illustration:

-   Business Strategy: How can a specific business unit (e.g., Siemens
    Healthcare --- diagnostic imaging) gain competitive advantage within
    its industry? The focus is on a single business competing against
    rivals in a defined market.

-   Corporate Strategy: How can the parent company (Siemens) obtain a
    \"parenting advantage\" --- that is, how can the whole be worth more
    than the sum of its parts? The test is whether V(Siemens) \> Σ
    V(Business Units). Corporate strategy asks how the parent adds value
    across its portfolio through shared capabilities, financial
    synergies, or strategic coordination.

Components of Strategy (Hambrick and Fredrickson, 2001)

Strategy is not a single decision but a set of five interdependent
elements forming a coherent whole:

-   Arenas --- Where will we be active? (Which markets, geographies,
    product categories, technologies, value-creation stages?)

-   Differentiators --- How will we win? (What will make us distinctive
    --- image, customization, price, reliability, speed?)

-   Vehicles --- How will we get there? (Internal development, joint
    ventures, licensing, acquisitions?)

-   Staging --- What will be our speed and sequence of moves? (Which
    initiatives come first? What is the logic of phasing?)

-   Economic Logic / Value Capture --- How will we obtain our returns?
    (Lowest costs through scale? Premium pricing through
    differentiation? Transaction revenues?)

Economic Logic sits at the center of the diamond --- it is the
integrating force that makes the other four elements coherent and
sustainable.

Course Philosophy

-   Stated goal: to change the way students think about strategy --- not
    just to teach tools.

-   Orientation: making strategy relevant and rigorous --- explicitly
    moving beyond simplistic frameworks like SWOT.

-   Commitment to customizing learning for students making important
    career transitions.

-   Strong belief in peer-to-peer learning: group work, discussion
    boards, and collaborative case analysis.

-   Weekly rhythm: concepts and tools → case analysis → interaction and
    organizational applications → video debriefs.

-   The \"Strategy Tool Matrix\" is used as a running organizer to
    connect concepts across weeks.

Recommended Reading

Richard P. Rumelt\'s The Crux: How Leaders Become Strategists is
recommended as supplementary reading. Rumelt is cited by McKinsey
Quarterly as a giant in the field of strategy, and the book focuses on
how leaders develop the diagnostic and problem-framing skills that
distinguish great strategists.
