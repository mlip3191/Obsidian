Disruptive Innovation Model --- Mukund Chari

Overview

This slide deck by Mukund Chari presents the Disruptive Innovation
Model, based on Clayton Christensen\'s seminal work. It explains why
technologically capable incumbents are routinely displaced by smaller,
seemingly inferior new entrants --- and offers strategic guidance for
how established firms and new entrants should manage innovation. The
deck uses disk drives, H&R Block/TurboTax, and the telecom industry as
illustrative cases.

The Three Key Elements of the Disruptive Innovation Model

1\. Customer Absorption Rate

In every market there is a rate of product improvement that customers
can actually absorb and utilize. For example, in the disk drive market,
customers could only use storage up to a certain level of capacity ---
beyond that threshold, additional storage provided diminishing value to
most users. This creates a performance ceiling that separates the needs
of different customer tiers.

2\. Technological Trajectories

Companies follow a technological trajectory as they innovate, and this
rate often exceeds what customers can actually use. Firms pursue better
and better products that yield higher margins when sold to the most
technologically demanding customers at the high end of the market. Over
time, this drives a wedge between what most customers need and what
established firms are developing.

3\. Sustaining vs. Disruptive Innovations

Sustaining innovations target the most technologically demanding
customers. Established firms win this game because they have:

-   Slack resources to invest in improvement

-   Existing customer relationships that define \"good\" products

-   Overhead structures requiring higher margins

-   Capital budgeting processes aligned to continuous improvement of
    existing technologies

Disruptive innovations are initially inferior on key performance
dimensions but offer other benefits --- simplicity, convenience, lower
cost, smaller size. New entrants win this game because they:

-   Have no existing customer base to protect or satisfy

-   Are comfortable operating in small, low-margin, uncertain markets

-   Lack large overhead that requires high margins to sustain

The Innovator\'s Dilemma

Disruptive technologies follow a predictable pattern:

-   Initially ignored: The technology underperforms on key metrics
    valued by mainstream and high-end customers, so incumbents
    rationally dismiss it.

-   Gradual improvement: The disruptive technology improves along its
    own trajectory, eventually intersecting with the needs of mainstream
    customers.

-   David beats Goliath: At the intersection point, the disruptive
    entrant has built cost structures, processes, and capabilities
    perfectly suited to the new standard --- while the incumbent is
    optimized for the old one.

The paradox --- the Innovator\'s Dilemma --- is that the incumbent\'s
past success is precisely what causes its failure. Listening to
customers, focusing on margins, and pursuing continuous improvement are
all rational, \"good\" management behaviors that make firms vulnerable
to disruption.

How to Counter Disruption

Early Warning Signals

Companies should monitor signals along the new product introduction
timeline --- from the earliest weak signals to commercial launch:

-   Discussions (weakest signal, earliest stage)

-   Scientific papers

-   R&D alliances

-   Patents

-   Process development

-   Product announced

-   Product sales (strongest signal, latest stage)

Early detection --- within the \"circled zone\" of discussions through
patents --- provides the longest window for strategic response. Waiting
until product announcements or sales leaves little time to respond.

Gartner\'s Hype Cycle

A complementary tool for tracking how new technologies evolve in market
expectations over time, passing through five stages:

-   Innovation Trigger: Technology emerges; early proof-of-concept
    stories generate press coverage

-   Peak of Inflated Expectations: Early publicity produces
    over-enthusiasm and unrealistic expectations

-   Trough of Disillusionment: Interest wanes as experiments and
    implementations fail to deliver

-   Slope of Enlightenment: More nuanced understanding develops; second-
    and third-generation products appear

-   Plateau of Productivity: Mainstream adoption occurs; applicability
    and relevance become widely accepted

Disrupt Yourself Before Others Do

The H&R Block example: Intuit\'s TurboTax entered the tax preparation
market targeting the least demanding customers (simple returns) --- a
segment H&R Block\'s brick-and-mortar business ignored. TurboTax then
moved upmarket. H&R Block eventually launched TaxCut as a response, but
came late. The lesson: established firms should proactively launch
disruptive versions of their own products before competitors do.

If You Can\'t Beat Them, Join Them

The telecom example: When cellular technology disrupted landline
telephony, established telecoms (AT&T, BellSouth) could not defend
landlines. Their response was to acquire wireless companies (AT&T
acquired Cingular to become the new AT&T) --- pivoting into the
disruptive technology rather than defending the existing one.

Key Takeaways

-   The Innovator\'s Dilemma: Past success puts a firm at risk. The very
    behaviors that make firms successful make them vulnerable to
    disruption.

-   Sustaining innovations reinforce existing industry structures;
    disruptive innovations destroy them.

-   Incumbents can address the Innovator\'s Dilemma --- not through
    vague commitments to \"take risks\" or \"invest for the long term\"
    --- but through early detection of warning signals and through
    organizational responses (creating separate units to pursue
    disruptive opportunities free from existing margin and customer
    pressures).

-   New entrants win disruptive battles not because they are better
    managed, but because their lack of legacy customers and overhead
    gives them strategic freedom that incumbents lack.
