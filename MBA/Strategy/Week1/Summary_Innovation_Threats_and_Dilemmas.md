Innovation Threats and Dilemmas --- Mukund Chari

Overview

This slide deck by Mukund Chari examines the broader phenomenon of
creative destruction and innovation-driven industry disruption. It
provides historical evidence of how even celebrated \"excellent\"
companies fail, quantifies the accelerating pace of creative
destruction, traces the mechanics of disruption through the disk drive
industry case, and connects the Disruptive Innovation framework to Five
Forces industry analysis and newer strategic tools such as Value
Innovation (Blue Ocean Strategy).

The Fate of \'Excellent\' Companies

The deck opens with a powerful exhibit: companies cited as \"excellent\"
in Peters and Waterman\'s In Search of Excellence (1983), covering the
period 1961--1981. Their subsequent fates illustrate creative
destruction in action:

-   DEC --- Missed the PC boat; RIP

-   IBM --- Near death in the early 1990s

-   3M --- Still thriving

-   Amdahl --- IBM clone; RIP

-   Gould --- RIP

-   Westinghouse --- RIP

-   Rockwell --- Slimmed down

-   Eastman Kodak --- RIP

-   Boeing --- Lost out to Airbus; doing better

-   K Mart --- Think Walmart

-   Data General --- RIP

-   Wang Labs --- RIP (nice concert hall in Boston)

-   Polaroid --- RIP

-   United Technologies --- Doing OK

The lesson: excellence at one point in time offers no protection against
creative destruction. The very strategies that made these firms
excellent in one era became liabilities in the next.

Creative Destruction Is Accelerating

Quantitative evidence shows the pace of creative destruction has
dramatically increased:

-   Average tenure of companies in the S&P 500 fell from approximately
    60 years in 1920 to roughly 15 years by 2000.

-   S&P 500 turnover rate: 1.5% per year in 1920 → 10% per year by 2020.

This acceleration means that firms face increasing urgency in managing
innovation strategically. What once gave companies decades of stability
now provides only years.

The Problem of Creative Destruction --- Two Explanations

Conventional Wisdom

The standard explanation for why leading firms fail attributes failure
to bad management:

-   Bureaucracy and organizational inertia

-   Arrogance and complacency

-   Poor strategic planning

-   Myopic or short-term investments

-   Fear of cannibalization of existing products

The Innovator\'s Dilemma (Christensen)

A counterintuitive alternative: historical success leads to a firm\'s
demise precisely through good management behaviors:

-   Listening to customers --- who ask for improvements to existing
    products, not disruptive new ones

-   Listening to marketing --- which forecasts markets that don\'t yet
    exist as unattractive

-   Focusing on getting larger margins --- which pushes firms upmarket
    toward demanding customers

-   Continuous improvement of a technology for a given market --- which
    overshoots what most customers need

The central question: why are leading firms in an industry often
displaced by new entrants? The answer is not bad management --- it is
rational management applied in a context where disruption is underway.

Creative Destruction in Disk Drives --- A Detailed Case Study

The disk drive industry provides the most detailed empirical
illustration of disruptive innovation dynamics. The disruption followed
five identifiable steps:

Step 1: Disruptive Technologies Are Developed Inside Established Firms

-   Control Data and Memorex, the dominant 14-inch drive manufacturers,
    had been working on an 8-inch drive for two years before it came to
    market.

-   Seagate developed the first working prototypes of a 3.5-inch drive
    and created 80 models before seeking top management approval.

Step 2: Marketing Consults Existing Customers --- Who Reject the
Innovation

-   IBM had no interest in Seagate\'s 3.5-inch drives (IBM wanted drives
    with 60+ MB capacity, which early 3.5-inch drives could not
    provide).

-   This led to pessimistic internal sales forecasts when the project
    was evaluated --- causing established firms to deprioritize or kill
    the project.

Step 3: Established Firms Double Down on Existing Technology

-   Seagate invested heavily in sophisticated component technologies for
    its 5.25-inch disks: thin-film disks, voice-coil actuators, SCSI
    interfaces, etc.

-   This sustained innovation improved the existing product but widened
    the gap between what incumbents were building and what disruptive
    entrants were developing.

Step 4: New Companies Form and Find New Markets by Trial-and-Error

-   Conner Peripherals, a leader in 3.5-inch drives, was formed by
    disaffected former employees from Seagate and Miniscribe (5.25-inch
    manufacturers).

-   These new entrants found early markets in portable computers and
    other applications that incumbents had dismissed as too small and
    low-margin.

Step 5: New Companies Move Upmarket

-   Seagate (originally a 5.25-inch drive pioneer) had itself moved
    upmarket from desktop PCs into minicomputers, engineering
    workstations, and mainframe markets.

-   Seagate was then driven from its original desktop PC market by
    Conner and Quantum --- the 3.5-inch pioneers --- repeating the
    pattern it had once inflicted on the 14-inch drive incumbents.

The disk drive industry shows multiple overlapping waves of disruptive
innovation between 1975 and 1995, with successive generations (14-inch →
8-inch → 5.25-inch → 3.5-inch) each following the same five-step
pattern.

Bringing Innovation into Strategic Analysis

The deck concludes by connecting innovation analysis to traditional
strategy tools:

-   Five Forces Framework: How should innovation be explicitly addressed
    in industry analysis? Disruptive innovation changes the competitive
    dynamics that Five Forces captures --- new entrants, substitute
    products, and changes in supplier and buyer power all shift when
    disruption occurs.

-   Analyzing Competitors in Innovation Environments: How do firms
    analyze actual and potential competitors when technological
    trajectories are uncertain? Standard competitor analysis must be
    supplemented with innovation-focused tools.

Two tools are highlighted as complements to Five Forces for
innovation-intensive environments:

-   Disruptive Innovation (Christensen): Tracks technological
    trajectories and identifies when new entrants are poised to
    intersect with mainstream customer needs.

-   Value Innovation / Blue Ocean Strategy: Identifies how firms can
    create new market spaces by redefining value rather than competing
    on existing dimensions --- making incumbent strengths irrelevant.

Key Takeaways

-   Even companies celebrated for excellence are vulnerable --- past
    performance provides no protection against creative destruction.

-   Creative destruction is accelerating: S&P 500 tenure has fallen
    dramatically and turnover rates have increased sevenfold since 1920.

-   The root cause of incumbent failure in innovation environments is
    not bad management but the rational application of good management
    practices --- listening to existing customers and chasing higher
    margins --- in a context where disruptive innovation is underway.

-   The disk drive industry demonstrates the five-step disruption
    pattern precisely: internal development → customer rejection →
    incumbent investment in sustaining technology → new firm formation →
    upmarket encroachment.

-   Incumbents can survive and even lead disruption --- through early
    detection of signals (scientific papers, patents, R&D alliances
    before product launches) and through organizational innovation
    (separate units insulated from existing customer and margin
    pressures).

-   Disruption frameworks (Disruptive Innovation, Value Innovation) must
    be integrated with industry structure tools (Five Forces) for a
    complete picture of competitive dynamics in innovation-intensive
    environments.
