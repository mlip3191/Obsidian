Economic Foundations of Strategy --- Mukund Chari

Overview

This slide deck by Mukund Chari grounds strategy in microeconomics and
explains why firms generate different levels of economic performance.
The central question --- what explains performance differences across
firms? --- is examined through two complementary lenses: industry
structure (the external view, rooted in Industrial Organization
economics) and internal resources and capabilities (the resource-based
view). The deck bridges economic theory and strategic practice, offering
practical implications for analysts, entrepreneurs, consultants, and
executives.

The Core Question of Strategy

What explains the difference in the economic performance of firms? The
deck opens with ROIC data comparing Pharmacia (19.55%) and Southwest
Airlines (12.75%) to their respective industry averages (28.14% and
5.05%). This illustrates that performance is shaped by both
firm-specific factors and industry membership --- and sets up the two
main explanatory frameworks.

Industry-Level Profitability

A chart of selected U.S. industries shows dramatic ROIC variation, with
the economy-wide average at 11.6%:

-   High profitability: Prepackaged software, Pharmaceuticals,
    Semiconductors

-   Moderate profitability: Restaurants, Surgical instruments,
    Homebuilding, Telephone communications

-   Low profitability: Petroleum refining, Motor vehicles, Natural gas
    distribution

-   Very low profitability: Catalog and mail order, Steel works,
    Airlines

This establishes that some industries are structurally more profitable
than others --- industry membership matters for baseline performance
expectations.

The Econ 101 Baseline

In efficient markets, economic profits are competed away as prices fall
with market entry. A firm earning above-normal returns attracts new
entrants; supply increases, prices fall, and profits erode to zero.
Strategy is fundamentally about identifying and capitalizing on market
inefficiencies before competition erodes them --- it picks up where
microeconomics leaves off.

Industry Structure as a Source of Economic Profit

The Industrial Organization (IO) economics perspective argues that
industry structure matters most. Economic profits persist because of
barriers to competition --- entry barriers, switching costs, economies
of scale --- that prevent new firms from competing away above-normal
returns. Some industries are intrinsically more profitable than others
because these structural barriers are higher. This is the theoretical
foundation for Porter\'s Five Forces analysis.

Practical implications:

-   Analysts: What variables indicate how attractive an industry is?

-   Entrepreneurs: How can I determine if a market is attractive before
    launching a venture?

-   Consultants: How do innovation and competitive dynamics affect
    industry attractiveness?

-   Executives: How do I analyze which industries to enter or exit?

Within-Industry Profitability Differences

Even within the same industry, firms show dramatic performance
variation:

-   Pharmaceuticals (Average ROIC 2012--2023): Wide spread across
    Pfizer, Johnson & Johnson, Roche, Eli Lilly, Novo Nordisk, and
    Gilead Sciences --- despite all competing in the same industry.

-   Steel Industry (ROA 1973--1992): Nucor significantly outperformed
    LTV, USX, Armco, Inland Steel, and Oregon Steel across all revenue
    ranges.

This within-industry variation suggests that firm-specific factors ---
not just industry structure --- drive performance differences,
motivating the resource-based view.

Internal Resources and Capabilities as Sources of Economic Profit

The resource-based view (RBV) argues that internal resources and
capabilities matter more than industry structure. Firm profits arise due
to barriers to resource acquisition and imitation. Two mechanisms:

-   Cost Advantage: A firm with lower average cost (AC) and marginal
    cost (MC) curves can earn profits even when the market price equals
    competitors\' costs. Barriers to imitation --- proprietary
    processes, tacit knowledge, scale --- prevent rivals from
    replicating the cost advantage.

-   Demand Advantage: A firm that commands a higher willingness-to-pay
    from customers (due to brand, quality, or unique features) can
    charge higher prices (P2 \> P1) and earn profits above the
    competitive baseline.

Practical implications:

-   Analysts: How do I understand why some firms generate greater
    economic profits than others in the same industry?

-   Entrepreneurs: How do I develop a viable competitive position within
    an industry dominated by established firms?

-   Consultants: How do I help clients build a low-cost or
    differentiation advantage?

-   Executives: How do I identify and leverage unique resources and
    capabilities?

Two Main Sources of Superior Performance --- Synthesizing Framework

The deck synthesizes the two perspectives into a single framework:

-   Internal resources and skills → Differentiation position or Cost
    position → Competitive advantage

-   Industry structure → shapes the competitive environment and the
    baseline level of profitability

-   Competitive advantage + Industry structure → Firm profitability

Superior performance therefore requires both being in an attractive
industry (or making it more attractive) and having a distinctive
competitive position within that industry.

Key Lessons

-   Strategy picks up where microeconomics leaves off --- both product
    market imperfections (external) and resource barriers (internal)
    create and sustain above-normal returns.

-   The external story: product markets are rarely perfectly
    competitive; barriers to competition create profit opportunities
    that strategy can identify and exploit.

-   The internal story: firms have competitive advantages over rivals
    due to barriers to imitation; strategy develops and sustains firm
    capabilities.

-   Strategic analysis requires a general management perspective,
    strategic thinking (dealing with complexity, ambiguity, and
    rivalry), and appropriate analytical tools applied to ask and answer
    the right questions.
