**Hewlett-Packard: The Flight of the Kittyhawk (A)**

HBS Case Study 9-606-088 \| Clayton M. Christensen

Case Summary

Overview

This Harvard Business School case study, written by Clayton M.
Christensen, examines Hewlett-Packard\'s attempt to pioneer the world\'s
smallest hard disk drive --- the Kittyhawk --- between 1991 and 1994. It
is a rich illustration of the Innovator\'s Dilemma: a well-resourced,
well-managed team pursuing an emerging market that ultimately fails to
materialize as anticipated, while unexpected customers surface with very
different needs. The case ends with project leader Rick Seymour facing a
pivotal decision about whether to pivot, persevere, or abandon the
project entirely.

Background and Context

HP\'s Disk Memory Division (DMD)

HP\'s Disk Memory Division, based in Boise, Idaho, generated \$519
million in disk-drive revenue in 1992 --- a small fraction of HP\'s \$16
billion corporate revenue, and dwarfed by industry leaders IBM (\$4
billion) and Seagate (\$3 billion). DMD was profitable but a niche
player, focused on high-performance 5.25- and 3.5-inch drives for
engineering workstations and network servers. Its product line offered
substantially higher storage capacity than the industry norm.

The Genesis of the Kittyhawk

Bruce Spenner, DMD\'s general manager, was dissatisfied with HP\'s
marginal position in the disk-drive industry. Inspired by his belief
that handheld computers and other microprocessor-powered devices
represented an untapped frontier, he envisioned a radically miniaturized
drive that could transcend traditional computing markets. Following
HP\'s internal maxim --- \"Never take a fortified hill\" --- Spenner
bypassed the crowded 2.5-inch notebook-drive market and instead targeted
an entirely new architecture: a 1.3-inch drive that had never existed
before.

In early 1991, Spenner presented the concept directly to Dick Hackborn,
the executive VP of Computer Products. Shown several prototype boxes of
different sizes, Hackborn chose the smallest --- the 1.3-inch form
factor --- and the Kittyhawk project was born.

Project Setup and Team

Organizational Structure

Recognizing that the Kittyhawk would face internal resistance --- DMD\'s
R&D managers believed the project conflicted with the division\'s core
priorities in high-capacity drives --- Spenner deliberately separated
the team from the main division. The Kittyhawk team was relocated to
trailers at a remote corner of the DMD campus, functioning essentially
as an internal startup with entrepreneurial speed but HP\'s financial
and technical backing.

Team Composition and Culture

Spenner prioritized hiring risk-takers who were excited by market
potential rather than technical specifications. Program manager Rick
Seymour and marketing manager Jeff White led the core team. To reinforce
the team\'s distinct mandate, R&D manager David Woito required all
engineers to sign a creed before joining: \"I am going to build a small,
dumb, cheap disk drive!\" Two engineers refused and returned to the HP
mainstream.

Project Charter --- Five Goals

-   Introduce the Kittyhawk within 12 months from project start

-   Achieve break-even time (BET) of less than 36 months

-   Reach a \$100 million annual revenue rate within two years of launch

-   Be the first 1.3-inch drive on the market

-   Grow faster than the overall disk-drive market (\~35% revenue growth
    rate)

Market Strategy and Target Customers

Initial Market Discovery

One week into the project, Seymour and White attended the Consumer
Electronics Show in Chicago, deliberately bypassing the desktop and
notebook computer sections. At the Nintendo exhibition --- one of the
largest at the show --- they were struck by the storage possibilities
for game cartridges. A Nintendo marketing manager expressed strong
interest but emphasized that \"cheap\" meant approximately \$50 per
unit, and that Nintendo shipped roughly 1.5 million cartridges per day
during the Christmas season.

The Chosen Strategy: Mobile Computing as Beachhead

After evaluating five possible market categories (mobile information
technologies, communications technologies, consumer electronics,
automotive electronics, and new computer technology), the team chose to
target the mobile computing market --- specifically personal digital
assistants (PDAs) --- as their primary beachhead. The logic was that PDA
volume would allow HP to achieve the scale needed to eventually reach
the \$50 price point through high-volume manufacturing.

Their three-page strategy document captured the vision simply:

-   Lead the industry in the 1.3-inch form factor

-   Ride the mobile computing explosion to get to low cost

-   \"I\'ll sell you a drive for \$49.95\"

Key Design Specifications

Two customer-driven requirements shaped the Kittyhawk\'s design: a
three-foot drop survivability requirement (from Dayton Electronics,
making rugged PDAs for package delivery companies) and a minimum 20 MB
storage capacity. These led to three breakthrough technologies:

-   Glass disk substrate: replacing aluminum platters with ultra-thin
    glass that could be polished flat enough to pack data more densely

-   Integrated electronics: reducing the drive\'s chip count from the
    typical 20-30 chips in a 1.8-inch drive down to just 5 custom chips,
    reducing power, weight, and cost

-   Piezoelectric accelerometer: a proprietary six-axis shock sensor
    (similar in concept to an airbag sensor) that detected impending
    impact and protected data --- at a component cost of over \$10

Manufacturing was outsourced to Japan\'s Citizen Watch Corporation,
which built an automated production line with capacity for 150,000 units
per month.

Launch and Initial Reception

The Kittyhawk launched exactly on schedule in June 1992 --- 12 months
from project start. It measured 0.4 x 2.0 x 1.44 inches and weighed
approximately 1 ounce: roughly half the size and one-third the weight of
the recently introduced 1.8-inch drives. HP co-founder William Hewlett,
retired and rarely attending product launches, presided at the press
conference. The Kittyhawk received more press coverage than any
new-product announcement in HP\'s history, won multiple technology
awards, and became a pocket demonstration piece for CEO Lew Platt.

Initial OEM pricing was set at approximately \$250 per unit. By July
1992, the team had design wins with six PDA developers, including Apple,
IBM, and HP\'s own Corvallis Division. Projected demand from the PDA
market for the next two years exceeded 500,000 units --- on track to
meet Spenner\'s revenue and break-even targets.

Market Failure and Unexpected Customers

The PDA Market Collapses

Within months of launch, the anticipated PDA market began to unravel.
Enabling technologies that PDAs depended upon --- particularly
handwriting-recognition software and new integrated circuitry --- proved
inadequate. Every PDA manufacturer saw disappointing sales. Most
withdrew from the market. The handheld devices that did succeed (e.g.,
simpler, lower-cost organizers) satisfied their modest storage needs
with flash memory chips rather than disk drives.

Simultaneously, HP\'s own Corvallis Division dropped the Kittyhawk in
favor of Integral Peripherals\' 1.8-inch drive, concluding that the
1.3-inch form factor could not meet the long-term storage requirements
of its super-subnotebook computers --- even accounting for the planned
40 MB second-generation drive.

The Ambiguity Problem

Critically, there was never a single, clear signal that the PDA market
had permanently failed. Each customer setback was followed by a new,
credible opportunity that kept the team engaged. Apple stumbled with the
Newton; but a major industrial firm, Chicago Controls, designed the
Kittyhawk into a factory-floor pen-based device for statistical process
control. Microsoft was developing a compatible OS --- until Microsoft
announced its OS would require more than 43 MB of storage, derailing the
concept entirely.

Actual vs. Planned Customers

The customers who actually purchased Kittyhawk units were almost
entirely different from those originally targeted:

-   Japanese word processors (Kanji translation devices): 100,000 units
    --- the largest single segment

-   Personal digital assistants: 35,000 units

-   Digital cameras: 20,000 units

-   Cash registers (backup storage, shock-resistant): 8,000 units

-   Telecommunications switching systems: 2,000 units

What these actual customers had in common was that they valued the
Kittyhawk\'s ruggedness --- its shock-resistance --- rather than its
small size or computing pedigree. These were markets the team had never
originally targeted.

The \$50 Market Emerges

As the ruggedness-based customer base slowly coalesced, a second and
very different customer need also crystallized: a cheap, simple drive at
approximately \$50, with no shock-resistance features. Nintendo revealed
they had already designed an entertainment system with a Kittyhawk slot
--- but explicitly did not need the accelerometer, just 20 MB at \$50. A
fax machine manufacturer indicated it could use the Kittyhawk in its
standard product line --- at 5 million units per year --- if the price
hit \$48. White identified at least 10 companies with similar
propositions. The \$50 price point represented a hard floor that
Kittyhawk\'s current design could not reach, primarily due to the \$10+
accelerometer cost.

The Strategic Decision

In mid-1994, with Kittyhawk sales well below plan and a monthly review
with Spenner approaching, Seymour convened the core team to evaluate
three strategic options:

Option 1: Continue Pursuing Ruggedness-Based Applications

Continue targeting the niche markets where the Kittyhawk\'s
shock-resistance was the key differentiator (industrial PDAs, specialty
devices). The problem: ramp to scale would be slow and unpredictable,
dependent on each customer\'s own success in novel markets. The team
doubted this business could grow fast enough to retain HP management\'s
interest and investment.

Option 2: Pivot to a Superior 2.5-Inch Notebook Drive

Leverage the ruggedness and advanced electronics integration developed
for the Kittyhawk to create a premium 2.5-inch notebook drive. This
would mean competing directly against formidable incumbents --- Quantum,
Conner, Seagate, and Western Digital --- in the mainstream market. Some
team members believed HP could command a price premium for ruggedness
and carve out a profitable high-end position.

Option 3: Design a \$50 Drive (Team\'s Preferred Path)

Develop a radically simplified, low-cost 1.3-inch drive targeting the
large-volume consumer electronics and industrial markets that had
revealed themselves. The team felt most energized by this option.
Seymour noted: \"Designing a 40 MB 1.3-inch drive that could drop three
feet onto a concrete floor was no simple feat, either. The \$50 drive
would be a very different challenge, but it probably wouldn\'t be more
difficult.\" The core obstacle was engineering out the expensive
accelerometer while achieving the cost structure needed to reach \$50.

Key Lessons and Themes

The Innovator\'s Dilemma in Action

The Kittyhawk case is a textbook example of Christensen\'s Innovator\'s
Dilemma. HP did almost everything right by conventional standards:
executive sponsorship, a separated innovation team, aggressive
timelines, rigorous project management, and deep customer research. Yet
the market HP targeted did not materialize as expected. The customers
who actually bought the product revealed needs --- ruggedness, low cost
--- that differed substantially from the original design thesis.

The Limits of Market Research in Emerging Markets

The market research firm HP contracted to validate the PDA market
opportunity ended up echoing HP\'s own beliefs --- \"they came to
believe what we believed\" --- because in an emerging market, there are
no established customers to survey. Conventional market research
methodologies are poorly suited to evaluating disruptive or non-existent
markets. This reflects a broader limitation: customers in emerging
markets often cannot articulate needs for products they have never seen.

Emergent Strategy and Customer Discovery

The most promising path forward --- the \$50 low-cost drive for consumer
electronics --- was not the strategy the team started with. It emerged
only after two years in the market. Nintendo, fax machine manufacturers,
and other mass-market customers revealed a latent demand that pre-launch
research had not identified. This illustrates the importance of
remaining open to emergent strategy signals, especially in early-stage
markets.

The Ambiguity of Failure Signals

One of the case\'s most instructive features is that there was never a
clear \"stop\" signal. Each setback was followed by a new credible
opportunity that rationally justified continued investment. This pattern
--- hope perpetually around the corner --- is a recurring feature of
disruptive innovation contexts and makes the decision to pivot or
abandon especially difficult for managers operating inside the fog of
uncertainty.

Organizational Design for Innovation

Spenner\'s decision to physically and organizationally separate the
Kittyhawk team from DMD\'s mainstream operations was a deliberate
attempt to insulate the project from the division\'s existing resource
allocation processes, customer relationships, and margin expectations.
This structural separation --- an internal startup model --- reflects
the organizational prescription that Christensen would later articulate:
disruptive innovation units need different metrics, different customers,
and different management processes than the core business.
