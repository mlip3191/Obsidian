# Apple Inc. in 2015 — Case Summary

> Harvard Business School Case 9-715-456 | Authors: David B. Yoffie & Eric Baldwin

---
## Overview

This Harvard Business School case, written by Professor David Yoffie and Eric Baldwin, examines Apple at a critical inflection point: Tim Cook's first major strategic initiative (the Apple Watch) following Steve Jobs's death, and the broader question of whether Apple could sustain its extraordinary trajectory without its legendary founder.

---
## Apple's Historical Arc

**Founding through crisis (1976–1997).** Jobs and Wozniak built Apple from a garage startup into a PC industry leader, but competitive missteps — particularly refusing to license the Mac OS while IBM's open "Wintel" standard dominated — sent the company into a tailspin. Three successive CEOs (Sculley, Spindler, Amelio) each failed to reverse the decline, and Apple came within 90 days of bankruptcy before Jobs returned in 1997.

**The Jobs turnaround.** Jobs immediately imposed focus: he slashed 15 product lines down to four, killed the Mac cloning program, recruited Tim Cook to fix the supply chain, and reinvested in R&D. Crucially, he repositioned Apple not just as a computer company but as a premium lifestyle and design brand — embodied in the "Think Different" campaign and eventually the Apple retail stores, which became what one analyst called "the Nordstrom of technology."

---
## The Four Pillars of Apple's Growth Engine

### 1. The Macintosh

Jobs's "digital hub" strategy reframed the Mac as the center of a consumer's digital life — the device that connected and controlled cameras, music players, phones, and more. Four key moves supported this:

- A completely overhauled OS (OS X, based on UNIX) in 2001
- A switch from PowerPC to Intel chips in 2006, enabling thinner designs and the ability to run Windows
- Proprietary software (the iLife suite) to differentiate the experience
- Apple retail stores to let consumers experience the ecosystem firsthand

By 2014, Apple held 91% of the U.S. market for PCs priced above $1,000 and grew to 6.4% global PC market share — remarkable for a company that never competed on price.

### 2. The iPod and iTunes

The iPod (2001) was the first major step beyond the Mac. Jobs's genius was in the system design: keep the device simple, put the complexity in iTunes on the computer. The iTunes Music Store (2003) completed the loop — the first legal, easy-to-use per-song download service. This created an inverted razor-and-blade model: songs were nearly break-even, but they drove enormously profitable iPod hardware sales.

The iPod's economics were exceptional by consumer electronics standards (roughly 40% gross margins on the Nano). However, by 2014, iPod revenues had fallen to less than a quarter of their 2008 peak, killed first by smartphones integrating music players, and then by streaming services (Spotify, Pandora) eroding digital download revenue. iTunes Store revenue fell roughly 13% in the first half of 2014 alone.

### 3. The iPhone

Introduced in 2007, the iPhone is the case's central business phenomenon. Key strategic elements included:

- **Platform control:** iOS, a mobile adaptation of OS X, gave Apple end-to-end control of hardware and software
- **Carrier leverage:** Apple initially negotiated an unprecedented revenue-sharing deal with AT&T, giving Apple pricing control — a reversal of typical carrier power dynamics
- **The App Store (2008):** Jobs initially resisted third-party apps, but the App Store became transformative — 75 billion downloads by mid-2014, with Apple keeping a 30% cut of paid app revenues, generating $10.2 billion in iTunes/App Store revenue in FY2014
- **Supply chain dominance:** Apple was one of the world's largest purchasers of flash memory, and it designed its own ARM-based chips (after acquiring two chip design firms for ~$400M), manufactured by Samsung

The financial results were staggering. By Q4 2014, Apple captured **93% of the entire global handset industry's profits** with only about 15% unit market share. The iPhone 6 and 6 Plus, with larger screens matching Android competitors, sold 74.5 million units in the 2014 holiday quarter alone — record-breaking quarterly profits of $18 billion, the largest in corporate history at the time.

By 2015, the iPhone accounted for **69% of Apple's total revenue**, which was both a testament to its success and a significant strategic vulnerability.

### 4. The iPad

Launched in 2010, the iPad created an entirely new product category. More than 450,000 units sold in the first week. By end of 2014, Apple had cumulatively sold nearly 240 million iPads, building a $30 billion annual business.

However, the iPad's momentum had stalled badly. Four consecutive quarters of sales declines leading into 2015, with unit sales down 5% and revenues down 4% for FY2014. Contributing factors included:

- Market saturation (tablets last longer than phones; replacement cycles are long)
- Heavy competition — Android tablets held 70% market share by end of 2014
- Ambiguity about the use case (tablets were primarily consumption, not productivity devices), limiting enterprise penetration

Cook's response was a partnership with IBM to develop enterprise-grade iPad/iPhone apps and deploy IBM's sales force — targeting the 90% of Fortune 500 companies that had Apple devices but at low penetration rates.

## The Competitive Landscape

**Smartphones:** Android, Google's free and open platform, had reached 81.5% global market share by 2014 vs. Apple's 14.8%. Samsung was the volume leader with 24.5% share, but Apple and Samsung together took more than 100% of the industry's profits (other players were losing money). Xiaomi, the fast-growing Chinese low-cost competitor, was perhaps the most feared new entrant — tripling sales to 61 million units in 2014.

**PCs:** The industry was in secular decline — shipments fell 10% in 2013 and 2.1% in 2014. Lenovo, HP, and Dell held 51% of global PC share collectively. Average profit margins across major PC manufacturers had fallen below 3%.

**Tablets:** Android dominated at 67.6% share; iOS fell to 27.5%. Microsoft's Surface remained under 5% share despite significant investment.

---
## New Bets: Apple Watch and Apple Pay

**Apple Watch** (announced March 2015, shipping April 2015) was Cook's first major product initiative. Positioned as a timepiece, fitness tracker, and iPhone companion, it started at $349 and went up to $17,000 for a gold edition. Key risks included: daily charging requirement, dependency on newer iPhones, and an already crowded market with Samsung, Pebble, and Google's AndroidWear ecosystem. Total smartwatch industry sales in 2014 were only about $1.1 billion — tiny by Apple's standards.

**Apple Pay** launched simultaneously, targeting mobile payments. It used NFC technology, TouchID fingerprint authentication, and tokenization (no card numbers transmitted to merchants). Major banks and retailers signed on quickly. Cook claimed Apple Pay accounted for two-thirds of mobile payments at participating retailers by December 2014.

## Strategic Tensions and Key Questions

The case surfaces several enduring strategic tensions:

**Open vs. closed:** Jobs's default was always closed, proprietary systems — but the iPod's real takeoff required opening to Windows users, and the App Store required opening to third-party developers. Apple managed a "controlled openness" — open enough to build ecosystems, closed enough to maintain margins and experience quality.

**Focus vs. diversification:** Apple's product line remained extraordinarily narrow for a $700B company. This was a deliberate Jobs principle, and Cook maintained it. But with iPod and iPad declining, and the iPhone representing 69% of revenue, the concentration risk was acute.

**Premium positioning vs. market share:** Apple consistently chose margin over volume. In smartphones, 15% unit share but 93% of profits. In PCs, sub-10% global share but 91% of the premium segment. This strategy works beautifully in growth phases but is vulnerable when competitors close the quality gap (as Samsung had largely done in screen size).

**Platform ecosystem lock-in:** iCloud, the App Store, iMessage, and continuity across Mac/iPhone/iPad created powerful switching costs. But competitors (Google, Microsoft) were building comparable ecosystems, and Android's app store surpassed Apple's in total app count for the first time in 2014.

---
## The Central Strategic Question

The case closes with the question Cook himself had to be wrestling with: **Could the Apple Watch and Apple Pay replicate the transformative impact of the iPhone and iTunes?** The Watch entered a nascent market with unproven consumer demand, high price points, and genuine dependence on the iPhone. Apple Pay faced entrenched payment habits and competition from Google Wallet and others.

More broadly, the case forces students to ask whether Apple's extraordinary success was the product of a repeatable strategic system — premium hardware, proprietary software, ecosystem lock-in, supply chain excellence, retail experience — or whether it was inseparable from Jobs's singular product intuition. Cook had executed flawlessly on the machine Jobs built. The question was whether he could build the next machine.
